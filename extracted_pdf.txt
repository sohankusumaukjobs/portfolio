Abstract 
Wildfires represent a significant danger to ecosystems, economy, and human safety, with their 
occurrence increasing owing to climate change and anthropogenic activities.  Prompt and 
precise detection is crucial to mitigate damage and facilitate efficient emergency actions.  This 
study presents a deep learning-based wildfire detection system that incorporates sophisticated 
image preprocessing techniques, including Multi -Scale Retinex (MSR) and RGB -to-YCbCr 
color space translation, with the DenseNet121 archit ecture.  The Wildfire Dataset, consisting 
of various fire and non -fire photos, was utilized for model training and assessment.  
Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) identified significant difficulties including brightness 
fluctuation, class imbalance, and resolution discrepancies.  Results indicate that DenseNet with 
preprocessing attained an impressive 98.84% test accuracy, in contrast to 90.95% without 
preprocessing, reflecting an 8% increase.  The accuracy of the CNN model increased from 
82.89% to 92.11% foll owing preprocessing.  Preprocessing significantly improved contrast, 
feature visibility, and resilience, resulting in substantial decreases in misclassification rates.  
The results confirm that improving input quality by MSR and color space conversion dire ctly 
enhances classification performance, particularly under difficult visual situations like smoke, 
haze, and inadequate illumination.  This study enhances image -based wildfire detection 
capabilities, providing a more precise and dependable solution than conventional raw-image 
classification methods.

Section 1: Introduction 
Forests are essential for maintaining ecological equilibrium, sometimes referred to as the 
“lungs of the planet” due to their capacity to absorb CO₂ and emit O₂.  They promote 
biodiversity, manage hydrological cycles, protect crops from wind, and enhance n ational 
economies through resources and job opportunities.  These ecosystems are increasingly 
imperilled by wildfires—unrestrained natural calamities that have escalated in frequency and 
intensity due to climate change and anthropogenic activity. (Barmpoutis, P., et al,2020) 
Wildfires annually devastate millions of acres, adversely affecting wildlife, agricultural output, 
and human lives.  Prominent instances encompass the 2020 Australian bushfires and the 2018 
California wildfires.  Although climate change exacerbates wildfir e conditions, research 
indicates that around 85% of U.S. wildfires from 1992 to 2015 were attributable to human 
activity. (Bouguettaya, A., et al,2022) 
The essential factor in mitigating wildfire damage is prompt discovery.  Nevertheless, 
conventional technologies such as satellites and watchtowers are constrained by constraints in 
visibility, expense, and resolution  (Barmpoutis, P., et al,2020) .  This necessitates the 
development of novel and dependable technology for the early and precise detection of 
wildfires, facilitating prompt responses and reducing environmental and economic damage. 
1.1 Contemporary Challenges 
 Notwithstanding continuous developments, wildfire detection systems encounter several 
significant hurdles.  Satellite -based methods, despite their extensive coverage, can have 
inadequate spatial resolution and prolonged return intervals, hindering the detection of minor 
or nascent fires  particularly in the presence of cloud cover or deep forest canopies  
(Ororatech ,2023).  Ground -based techniques such as watchtowers and infrared cameras are 
constrained by visibility range, substantial installation expenses, and environmen tal 
disruptions, especially in adverse weather conditions or at night. 
Moreover, several systems depend on human oversight, which is susceptible to delays and 
inaccuracies resulting from exhaustion or insufficient real -time response.  Detection systems 
frequently encounter false positives, mistakenly identifying sunlight refl ections or industrial 
heat sources as genuine fires, so undermining faith and taxing emergency resources. (Johnston, 
J.M., et al, 2018) 
These concerns underscore the necessity for sophisticated, scalable, and accurate wildfire 
detection techniques.  This study mitigates these deficiencies by using picture preprocessing 
and DenseNet-based deep learning to improve classification accuracy and  reduce detection 
latency. 
1.2 Aim of the project

advanced picture preprocessing methods specifically Multi-Scale Retinex (MSR) and RGB-to-
YCbCr color space transformation alongside the DenseNet deep learning a rchitecture for 
classification.  This method aims to increase the quality of wildfire images, boost feature

Section 2: Literature review  
Wildfire detection is a vital study area propelled by the rising frequency and intensity of 
wildfires worldwide.  Conventional techniques, such as satellite images, heat sensors, and 
terrestrial watchtowers, frequently fail to provide prompt, high -resolution, and economical 
detection especially in environments influenced by smoke, haze, or variable illumination.  
Consequently, deep learning and computer vision methodologies have surfaced as viable 
options for automating wildfire identification using picture data. 
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) like ResNet, VGG, and MobileNet have been 
extensively utilized in the categorization of wildfire images.  Nevertheless, several models 
depend on unrefined or weakly refined pictures, hence constraining their robustness and 
precision in practical applications.  This approach mitigates that constraint by integrating 
sophisticated preprocessing techniques  Multi-Scale Retinex (MSR) for light correction and 
RGB-to-YCbCr color space transformation for improved fire feature isolation into a DenseNet-
based classification framework. 
2.1 Related works 
Wu, et al. (2020) introduced a wildfire detection system employing MobileNetV2 and transfer 
learning for rapid and precise picture categorization.  The model was trained using wildfire 
photos acquired by UAVs and included a bespoke data augmentation pipeline t hat simulated 
difficult circumstances such as Gaussian blur and noise.  The UAVs provide versatility, 
security, and economic efficiency relative to conventional detecting methods.  The collection 
comprised annotated aerial pictures featuring both fire and non-fire occurrences.  The model 
attained 96.7% accuracy on the original dataset, 95.3% on blurred photos, and 95.3% on images 
with Gaussian noise.  This exhibits significant generality and resilience across many visual 
contexts, highlighting its applicability for real -time wildfire monitoring systems in practical 
settings. 
Treneska, and Stojkoska,  (2021) employed transfer learning with ResNet50 to identify 
wildfires from photos captured by UAVs. Their methodology optimized many pre -trained 
CNNs, such as ResNet50, Xception, and InceptionV3, utilizing the FLAME dataset, which 
comprises approximately 3 9,000 annotated drone -captured photos of fire and non -fire 
scenarios. Fine -tuning ResNet50 achieved the highest classification performance with an 
accuracy of 88%, representing an 11% enhancement over baseline models. The researche rs 
established that transfer learning markedly improves generalization and is appropriate for real-
time wildfire detection. Their research underscored the increasing significance of UAV-based 
data acquisition, especially for high-resolution, preliminary fire monitoring applications. 
Ghali, et al. (2022) introduced an innovative ensemble wildfire detection system that integrates 
EfficientNet-B5, and vision transformers —TransUNet and TransFire —for classification and 
segmentation purposes.  Their dataset comprised UAV -acquired aerial photos, annotated for 
fire and non-fire areas.  The classification model attained an accuracy of 95.12%, These models 
tackled issues such as limited fire size, visual deterioration, and intricate backdrops.  This work

confirms the efficacy of integrating CNNs and transformers for precise fire localization and 
detection in UAV surveillance applications. 
Sathishkumar, et al.  (2023) employed transfer learning with VGG16, InceptionV3, and 
Xception, augmented by Learning without Forgetting (LwF) to preserve model efficacy on 
previous datasets. The research utilized the BowFire dataset for novel tasks and a distinct 
foundational dataset . The Xception model achieved 79.23% accuracy on BowFire without 
LwF. Utilizing LwF, the performance enhanced to 91.41% while preserving 96.89% on the 
original dataset. This methodology mitigates catastrophic forgetting in transfer learning and is 
effective for adapting wildfire models to novel geographies or data distributions. It is especially 
advantageous when new training data is scarce, while preserving historical performance is 
essential. 
The CT -Fire model, developed by Ghali, and Akhloufi,  (2023), is a hybrid technique that 
integrates CNN and Transformer architectures, specifically utilizing RegNetY and 
EfficientFormer v2. The training and testing utilized a combination of terrestrial and airborne 
wildfire datasets: FLAME, CorsicanFire, DeepFire, and Fire. CT -Fire attained 9 7.62% 
accuracy on terrestrial pictures and 87.77% on aerial photos, surpassing benchmark CNNs and 
transformers. The ensemble exhibited resistance to intricate backdrops,  minor burning areas, 
and fluctuating fire intensities. This study demonstrates that the integration of CNNs and 
transformers effectively captures both local and global picture data, rendering it appropriate 
for thorough wildfire detection across diverse imaging situations. 
Wang, et al, ( 2023) developed two wildfire detection modules: a Reduce -VGGNet for 
classification and an improved CNN that integrates spatial and temporal information for 
regional detection. The research sought to reconcile performance with accessibility in 
educational settings. The project utilized a selected wildfire dataset appropriate for Machine 
Vision course applications. The Reduce-VGGNet attained a classification accuracy of 91.20%, 
but the spatio-temporal CNN acquired an accuracy of 97.35% in region detection. This modular 
methodology delivers both scholarly and practical benefits, presenting a dependable detection 
system characterized by high precision, while being appropriate for educational purposes. 
Jonnalagadda, and Hashim, (2024) presented SegNet, a segmented convolutional neural 
network designed for real -time wildfire identification utilizing drone (UAV) footage. The 
approach emphasizes enhancing processing performance by minimizing superfluous feature 
maps while preserving pixel-level accuracy for amorphous entities such as fire and smoke. A 
bespoke UAV wildfire dataset was utilized, obtained through drone flights over fire -prone 
regions. SegNet markedly enhanced inference speed and attained exceptionally high detection 
accuracy; although the pre cise numerical precision was not specified, it outperformed 
conventional CNN methods in both performance and computing economy. This research offers 
a significant addition to real -time wildfire monitoring, particularly in bandwidth -limited or 
mobile environments. 
Yandouzi et al. utilized deep transfer learning with several pre -trained CNN architectures, 
including VGG16, ResNet50, Xception, MobileNet, and NASNetMobile, for the detection of 
forest fires. The scientists trained their models using a composite wildfire dataset obtained from

attained even with very limited training datasets. This renders it appropriate for practical 
applications using constrained labelled wildfire data. 
2.2 Critical Assessment of existing Literature 
The analysed papers together demonstrate substantial advancements in wildfire detection via 
the implementation of deep learning, specifically utilizing transfer learning and CNN -based 
frameworks.  Numerous studies, including those by Wu et al. (2020), Treneska and Stojkoska 
(2021), and Ghali et al. (2022), illustrate that pretrained models such as MobileNetV2, 
ResNet50, and EfficientNetB5 are very proficient when fine-tuned for fire classification tasks.  
The use of vision transformers, shown by CT -Fire and TransFire, improves spatial awareness 
and feature representation, facilitating precise detection in congested or intricate visual 
environments.  Numerous models, such those introduced by Wang et al. (2023) and 
Jonnalagadda and Hashim (2024), emphasize com puting efficiency, rendering them 
appropriate for real -time UAV deployment.  Moreover, techniques like as Learning without 
Forgetting (LwF) demonstrate potential in preserving knowledge during cross -domain 
adaptation, and research by Yandouzi et al. (2022) validates that deep models may maintain 
remarkable performance while having minimal labeled data. 
Notwithstanding these developments, a significant drawback in most investigations is the 
inadequate application of sophisticated picture preparation techniques.  Although several 
articles utilize fundamental data augmentation techniques (e.g., rotations, f lips, noise), few 
include more sophisticated approaches like as denoising filters, contrast improvement, Retinex 
algorithms, or color space transformations.  This absence may diminish model robustness in 
real-world scenarios marked by smoke, haze, low illu mination, or inadequate contrast.  
Numerous approaches depend significantly on architectural complexity to get performance 
improvements, which, although beneficial in controlled datasets, may not translate effectively 
to noisy or damaged field data.  Research conducted by Wu et al. (2020) and Ghali et al. (2022) 
demonstrates commendable performance on pristine datasets; nevertheless, there is little 
exploration into the potential enhancements in accuracy and resilience through preprocessing 
techniques.  The prevailing tendency indicates a disparity in the synergy between the increase 
of input quality and classification, highlighting an opportunity for additional investigation such 
as this project  which seeks to rectify this constraint by incorporating certain preprocessing 
techniques with deep learning models. 
2.3 Gaps Identified in the Literature 
Current work on wildfire detection via deep learning has achieved significant advancements; 
nonetheless, notable gaps persist, especially concerning picture preprocessing and the 
equilibrium between model complexity and efficiency.  This study tackles thes e deficiencies 
by employing an innovative pipeline that combines sophisticated preprocessing with an 
efficient, lightweight model.

Insufficient Advanced Image Preprocessing Techniques:  Numerous research depends on 
fundamental data augmentation methods such as rotation, flipping, and Gaussian noise (Govil, 
et al 2020) .  Nonetheless, these methodologies fail to sufficiently equip models for practical 
situations characterized by haze, smoke, or inadequate lighting. This effort mitigates this 
deficiency by employing Multi-Scale Retinex (MSR) for lighting correction and transforming 
RGB to YCbCr color space, therefore markedly improving picture clarity and contrast, which 
renders the fire zones more discernible prior to classification. 
Excessive Dependence on Deep Architectures Without Input Augmentation: Architectural 
advancements like TransUNet or CT-Fire prioritize model depth at the expense of input quality. 
This research integrates a somewhat intricate yet effective architecture (DenseNet) with 
specific preprocessing, demonstrating that input augment ation can increase accuracy without 
necessitating computationally intensive model layers. (Jain, et al 2020) 
Insufficient Model Efficiency in Real -Time Contexts: Numerous high-performing models 
are computationally demanding and inappropriate for real-time or embedded implementation. 
( Saleh, et al,2024) 
This project utilizes DenseNet’s  efficiency, since its dense connection minimizes duplicate 
calculations and enhances feature reuse.  This guarantees rapid, memory -efficient inference, 
rendering it optimal for real-time UAV-based wildfire surveillance systems. 
2.4 Relation to Research and Hypothesis 
The examined literature demonstrates substantial advancements in wildfire identification 
utilizing deep learning and images obtained from UAVs.  Research conducted by Wu et al. 
(2020), Treneska and Stojkoska (2021), and Ghali et al. (2022) has effectively utilized transfer 
learning methodologies on CNN architectures, including MobileNetV2, ResNet50, and 
EfficientNet-B5, to achieve significant accuracy in wildfire detection.  Other models, such as 
CT-Fire and SegNet, have prioritized architectural innovation  and computing efficiency to 
satisfy real-time operating requirements. 
Nonetheless, a significant weakness in these investigations is the inadequate emphasis on 
improving the visual quality of input data by sophisticated preprocessing approaches.  Many 
models predominantly depend on data augmentation or increased architectura l depth to 
enhance performance, neglecting the advantages of techniques like as denoising, illumination 
correction, or color space transformation in intricate, real -world settings characterized by 
smoke, haze, and inadequate lighting. 
This study directly tackles this deficiency by including picture preprocessing techniques —
specifically Multi-Scale Retinex (MSR) and RGB-to-YCbCr color space transformation—into 
the classification pipeline.  The modifications are designed to enhance pictur e clarity and 
feature differentiation prior to processing by the DenseNet model, recognized for its effective 
feature propagation and reutilization.

Section 3: Methodology of the project 
This section delineates the methodical strategy employed to create an effective and resilient 
wildfire detection system utilizing deep learning, along with sophisticated image preprocessing 
techniques.  The methodology aims to improve image clarity and cla ssification precision in 
real-world visual problems, including smoke, haze, and variable lighting —conditions 
frequently inadequately represented in current models. 
The workflow, depicted in Figure 1, commences with data collection, succeeded by exploratory 
data analysis (EDA) to comprehend data distribution, detect class imbalances, and visualize 
sample attributes.  Thereafter, the raw pictures undergo preprocessing through RGB-to-YCbCr 
color space translation to improve the distinction between fire and non-fire features, succeeded 
by Multi -Scale Retinex (MSR) processing for illumination normalization and contrast 
augmentation. 
Upon completion of preprocessing, the augmented photos are utilized for model construction, 
wherein the DenseNet architecture is trained to classify the existence of wildfires.  The model 
is subjected to hyperparameter adjustment to enhance performance.  T he concluding phase 
entails performance assessment utilizing metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 -
score to evaluate model efficacy under diverse environmental settings.

3.4 Data preprocessing: Multi-Scale Retinex (MSR) 
The Multi -Scale Retinex (MSR) algorithm is a sophisticated picture enhancement method 
aimed at improving visual perception by rectifying uneven illumination and augmenting details

conditions.  MSR enhances dynamic range compression, color consistency, and detail 
enhancement, rendering it exceptionally beneficial for wildfire detection, where flames, smoke, 
and background settings exhibit significant variability in visibility.

Implementation of the algorithm: 
The Python implementation of the Multi -Scale Retinex  (MSR) algorithm initiates by 
converting the input image to a float32 data type and incrementing each pixel value by 1 to 
avert mathematical complications associated with the logarithm of zero.  A Gaussian blur is 
applied to simulate the illumination compo nent of the image for each designated sigma value

illumination.  T he data are aggregated across all scales and averaged to get the final MSR -
enhanced image.  The output is ultimately adjusted to the 0–255 range and transformed into an 
8-bit format appropriate for visualization and model input.  This implementation enable s the 
model to adeptly manage varied lighting conditions in wildfire photos, hence enhancing 
visibility and accentuating essential elements vital for precise categorization.

3.6 Classification model: DenseNet Model 
DenseNet (Densely Connected Convolutional Network) is a cutting-edge convolutional neural 
network architecture recognized for its efficiency and superior performance in image 
classification applications.  Huang et al. (2017) proposed DenseNet, which has an innovat ive 
connectivity structure wherein each layer obtains inputs from all prior layers and transmits its 
own feature maps to all subsequent layers.  This intricately linked architecture enhances 
information transmission and feature reutilization across the net work, mitigating vanishing 
gradient problems, improving feature propagation, and substantially decreasing the parameter 
count. 
Architecture of DenseNet 
DenseNet121 is a deep convolutional neural network consisting of 121 layers, characterized 
by a dense connection architecture in which each layer receives inputs from all preceding layers 
within the same block  (figure 12).  This architecture facilitates feature reutilization, gradient 

======PAGE======
29 
 
propagation, and parameter efficiency.  The architecture commences with a 7×7 convolution 
and 3×3 max pooling, succeeded by four dense blocks comprising 6, 12, 24, and 16 layers 
correspondingly, each interspersed with transition layers that incorporate 1×1 convolutions and 
average pooling.  The model terminates with global average pooling followed by a fully linked 
layer.  Exhibiting a growth rate of 32, it effectively produces new features while preserving a 
manageable model size of approximately 8 million parameters. (Huang et al. 2017) 
 
Figure 12: Architecture of DenseNet. 
Proposed Architecture 
This project involved freezing the pretrained DenseNet121 backbone and augmenting it with 
custom layers, which included global average pooling, a 256 -unit dense layer, dropout (0.2), 
and a sigmoid classifier (figure 13).  The base model is initialized with ImageNet weights and 
remains fixed during training to preserve pretrained feature representations.  A Global Average 
Pooling layer is incorporated atop the DenseNet base to diminish the spatial dimensions of the 
feature maps, succeeded by a completely linked dense layer utilizing ReLU activation to create 
non-linearity.  A dropout layer is utilized for regularization, and a concluding sigmoid -
activated output layer is employed for binary classification (fire versus no fire).

 This combination attained a validation accuracy of 97.5%, signifying exceptional

The model was trained on processed wildfire pictures. The preprocessed inputs comprised

preceding layers, hence enhancing generalization (Huang et al. 2017).  Pretrained on ImageNet, 
it offers a robust feature extraction framework, minimizing training duration and enhancing 
performance in transfer learning contexts.  The model's compact dimensions and depth provide

time applications such as wildfire detection in diverse climatic situations, where precision and

Explanation of t-SNE 
 t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor  Embedding (t -SNE) is a non -linear dimensionality 
reduction method commonly employed for displaying high -dimensional data in reduced 
dimensions, generally two or three dimensions.  t-SNE, created by Laurens van der Maaten and 
Geoffrey Hinton, transforms similarities among data points into joint probabilities and aims to 
reduce the divergence between these probabilities in the low-dimensional representation. 
 
 
The technique excels in maintaining the local structure of the data, indicating that points in 
high-dimensional space that are proximate tend to remain near in the reduced depiction.  t-SNE 
is exceptionally useful for detecting clusters, patterns, and correlations that are not readily 
apparent in high-dimensional datasets. 
This experiment employed t -SNE to depict the distinguishability between fire and non -fire 
photos based on extracted attributes such as brightness, contrast, and texture metrics.  It enabled 
a visual assessment of the potential for the characteristics to in herently create discernible 
clusters prior to categorization.  While t-SNE is not a classifier, it demonstrated the significance 
of preprocessing and model selection by revealing incomplete grouping and overlapping 
regions, hence underscoring the necessity for deep learning-based feature extraction.

Evaluation Methods for MSR: Entropy and Contrast 
1. Shannon Entropy 
Formula: 
 
Entropy quantifies the degree of information or randomness inherent in a picture. An elevated 
entropy value signifies enhanced detail, improved texture visibility, and greater variety in pixel 
intensities. 
The rationale for its use: In wildfire detection, it is essential to capture intricate features such 
as flame peripheries and smoke gradients. The increased entropy during MSR preprocessing 
indicates that certain critical characteristics are retained and even augmented, rendering the 
picture more useful for the DenseNet model. 
2. Detection of Low Contrast 
The exposure.is_low_contrast function assesses if a picture exhibits inadequate contrast by 
analyzing the distribution of pixel intensities.  It returns True if the image's intensity range is 
below a defined threshold (e.g., 5%). 
The rationale for its use: Wildfire imagery frequently experiences smoke and illumination 
irregularities, resulting in diminished contrast.  The objective of assessing contrast was to 
confirm that MSR enhanced detail (via entropy) while preventing the gene ration of pictures 
with faded or indistinct details.  A "low contrast penalty" was employed during MSR tuning to 
emphasize crisper, sharper outputs, hence enhancing the model's reliability in fire detection.

Baseline CNN Model Explanation 
• Conv2D Layer (Convolutional Layer): To autonomously acquire spatial hierarchies of 
features from input pictures. Implements filters throughout the picture to identify 
characteristics like as edges, textures, and patterns; each filter acquires distinct feat ure 
maps throughout the training process. 
• Activation (ReLU): Introduce non -linearity to facilitate the learning of intricate 
patterns. Transforms input values to output in a non -linear manner, allowing the 
network to represent more intricate relationships. 
• MaxPooling2D Layer (Pooling Layer): Decrease the spatial dimensions of feature maps 
to minimize computation and mitigate overfitting. Extracts the greatest value from a 
compact window (e.g., 2×2) inside the feature map, preserving essential features while 
minimizing dimensions. 
• Flatten Layer: Convert 2D feature maps into a 1D feature vector for dense layers. 
Transforms multidimensional feature maps into a singular vector appropriate for fully 
linked layers. 
• Dense Layer (Fully Connected Layer): Acquire advanced patterns and execute 
decision-making. Each neuron is interconnected with all neurons in the preceding layer 
to acquire intricate feature correlations. 
• Dropout Layer: Mitigate overfitting and enhance model generalization. Randomly 
disables a fraction of neurons during training to enhance the model's robustness. 
 
Architecture used: 
The basic Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model adheres to a conventional design 
centered on feature extraction and classification, as seen in the picture above. 
The components utilized in the model are: 
Convolutional Layers 
• Conv2D (32 filters, 3×3 kernel, ReLU activation): Extracts fundamental characteristics, 
including edges and textures, from the input picture (224×224×3).

such as forms and patterns. 
• Conv2D (128 filters, 3×3 kernel, ReLU activation): Extracts advanced features that

 Pooling Layers
